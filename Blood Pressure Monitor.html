Blood Pressure Monitor

March 2023

Using knowledge of mechatronics, python, and pysiology to design and build a fully functional blood pressure monitor.

Skills:Medical Device Design, Python, Circuit Design, Experimental Design, Mechatronics

Project Goal

The goal of this project was to design a circuit using a Honeywell gague pressure transducer and write code that outputs the blood pressure of a patient. The design of the circuit was then compared to on market blood pressure sensors using statistical analysis.

The goal of the physical filters and the code is to transition from the raw data of pressure vs time (left) to numerical outputs of blood pressure and heart rate.


Circuit Overview

The circuit featured an initial amplifier to increase the impedence throghout. The signal was then fed through a high pass filter designed to filter out any low frequency noise such as the variation in pressure caused by pumping. Another amplifier came before a second filter. The signal is then fed through a low pass filter designed to filter out noise with high frequency such as that caused by movement.




The pressure readings before the signal is filtered through the circuit are very similar to the expected output of the cuff. Blood pressure cannot be directly taken off of this graph.





After filtration, the blood pressure graph looses information about the total pressure but adds details about the oscillations. Blood pressure also cannot be directly taken off of this graph but it provides more of the information required to calculate it. The second graph has also been trimmed along the time axis to the relevant data.


Code Overview

The code is used to transform the pressure vs time readings output by the circuit into blood pressure. There are three elements to blood pressure readings. Systolic pressure is the amount of pressure in the arteries when the heart is contracted. Diastolic pressure is the amount of pressure in the arteries when the heart is relaxed. Mean arterial presssure is the average pressure during one cardiac cycle.

The mean artirial pressure (MAP) is found at the peak of the largest oscillation of pressure (indicated by the center red line below). Systolic pressure is found at the peak where the magnitude of the oscillation corresponds to 55% of the maximum oscillation (indicated by the left most green line). Diastolic pressure occures at the peak corresponding to when the pressure has decreased to 70% of the maximum ocsillation magnitude (right most green line).

The figure above consists of three graphs: the output directly from the instrumentational amplifier (top), the output from the band pass filter (middle), and the magnitude of the pressure ocillation peaks (bottom).

The peaks must be identified to find any blood pressure numbers. Peaks were identified using the python peak finder function with parameters on threshold, prominence, and distance (shown left).





The mean arterial pressure of this data set is calculated using a python algerithm and is output to be 93.08 mmHg. Similarly, diastolic pressure was calculated to be 62.88 mmHg and systolic pressure was calculated to be 127.44 mmHg. These numbers reflect a slightly elevated blood pressure which is accurate for the subject they were taken from.
